# Export Controls and Semiconductor Geopolitics

Export controls are one of the main instruments shaping competition in the global semiconductor industry.

They affect access to advanced chips, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, software, and critical inputs. As a result, export controls have become a central tool of economic security and technological containment.

---

## Why Export Controls Matter

In the semiconductor sector, export controls can:

- restrict access to advanced manufacturing capabilities
- slow down technological progress in rival states
- limit access to semiconductor equipment and design tools
- reshape supply chains and industrial strategies
- increase geopolitical and commercial uncertainty

---

## Main Areas of Restriction

### Advanced Chips

Restrictions often target:

- high-performance AI chips
- advanced GPUs
- chips used in supercomputing
- dual-use semiconductors with military relevance

### Semiconductor Manufacturing Equipment

Restrictions may affect:

- EUV and advanced DUV lithography systems
- etching and deposition equipment
- inspection and metrology tools
- advanced wafer fabrication machinery

### Software and Design Tools

Controls can also apply to:

- electronic design automation (EDA) software
- advanced chip design tools
- manufacturing process software

### Materials and Components

Some restrictions may affect:

- specialty chemicals
- critical minerals
- semiconductor-grade materials
- industrial inputs with dual-use relevance

---

## Key Actors

### United States

The United States plays a leading role in semiconductor export controls through its technological, regulatory, and alliance-based influence.

**Strategic objectives:**
- preserve technological leadership
- restrict rivals' access to advanced capabilities
- protect national security interests
- reinforce domestic and allied semiconductor capacity

---

### China

China is one of the main targets of semiconductor export controls, especially in advanced manufacturing and AI-related technologies.

**Strategic implications:**
- pressure to develop domestic alternatives
- acceleration of self-sufficiency efforts
- increased investment in local foundries, tools, and materials

---

### Netherlands

The Netherlands is strategically important because of its role in semiconductor equipment, especially lithography.

**Strategic relevance:**
- home to ASML
- key node in allied export control coordination
- central to restrictions on advanced lithography access

---

### Japan

Japan is also a critical actor due to its role in semiconductor materials, equipment, and specialty chemicals.

**Strategic relevance:**
- supplier of key production inputs
- important participant in technology control coordination

---

## OSINT Indicators to Monitor

When tracking export controls, key indicators include:

- new government regulations and licensing rules
- company disclosures about restricted sales
- public statements by commerce or trade ministries
- changes in equipment delivery permissions
- sanctions or entity-list designations
- industrial policy responses by affected countries
- announcements of domestic substitution programs

---

## Analytical Implications

Export controls can generate several second-order effects:

- acceleration of domestic industrial policy
- supply chain fragmentation
- growth of parallel technology ecosystems
- pressure on allied coordination
- increased costs for global semiconductor firms

---

## Key Risk Questions

Analysts should monitor:

- Which technologies are being restricted?
- Which countries or firms are targeted?
- Which chokepoints are most exposed?
- How are affected actors adapting?
- What are the implications for supply chain resilience?

---

## Strategic Relevance

Export controls are not only trade tools. In the semiconductor sector, they function as instruments of:

- economic security
- technological competition
- alliance coordination
- geopolitical leverage

They are now one of the defining features of the global semiconductor landscape.
