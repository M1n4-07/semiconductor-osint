# Industrial Policy and the Semiconductor Sector

Industrial policy has become a central feature of the global semiconductor industry.

Governments increasingly use subsidies, tax incentives, public-private partnerships, and strategic regulation to strengthen domestic semiconductor capabilities and reduce dependence on external suppliers.

---

## Why Industrial Policy Matters

Semiconductors are now seen as strategic assets because they are essential for:

- economic competitiveness
- digital infrastructure
- military and dual-use technologies
- artificial intelligence
- critical supply chain resilience

As a result, governments are treating semiconductor production as a matter of economic security and national strategy.

---

## Main Policy Objectives

Industrial policy in semiconductors usually aims to:

- expand domestic manufacturing capacity
- attract foreign investment in fabs
- reduce supply chain vulnerabilities
- support research and development
- strengthen talent and workforce pipelines
- build resilience in critical inputs and equipment

---

## Main Policy Tools

### Subsidies and Public Funding

Governments provide financial support for:

- fab construction
- equipment acquisition
- R&D programs
- advanced packaging and testing capacity

### Tax Incentives

These may include:

- investment tax credits
- corporate tax reductions
- accelerated depreciation for capital expenditure

### Strategic Regulation

Governments may also use:

- export controls
- local content requirements
- foreign investment screening
- national security reviews

### Public-Private Partnerships

States often cooperate with:

- foundries
- equipment manufacturers
- universities
- research institutes

---

## Key Geographic Hubs

### United States

The United States has prioritized semiconductor reshoring and advanced manufacturing capacity.

**Strategic goals:**
- reduce dependence on Asian manufacturing
- strengthen national security
- support domestic innovation
- preserve technological leadership

---

### European Union

The European Union has focused on strategic autonomy and industrial resilience.

**Strategic goals:**
- increase Europe’s share of semiconductor production
- reduce external dependencies
- attract large-scale investment in fabs and materials

---

### China

China has developed a long-term strategy to reduce dependence on foreign semiconductor technologies.

**Strategic goals:**
- increase self-sufficiency
- build domestic champions
- expand local production of chips, tools, and materials

---

### Japan

Japan has re-emerged as an important actor through support for materials, equipment, and advanced semiconductor partnerships.

**Strategic goals:**
- rebuild advanced chip capacity
- strengthen strategic industries
- deepen cooperation with allies

---

### South Korea and Taiwan

Both remain central to global semiconductor production and continue to support their industries through strong state-industry coordination.

**Strategic goals:**
- maintain technological leadership
- defend core industrial advantages
- secure strategic relevance in global supply chains

---

## OSINT Indicators to Monitor

When tracking industrial policy, key indicators include:

- subsidy announcements
- new fab investments
- tax incentive packages
- public-private partnership agreements
- industrial strategy documents
- national security reviews of semiconductor investments
- talent and workforce development programs

---

## Analytical Implications

Industrial policy can reshape the semiconductor industry by:

- relocating production capacity
- increasing competition for investment
- fragmenting supply chains
- creating new strategic hubs
- reinforcing alliances and economic blocs

---

## Key Risk Questions

Analysts should monitor:

- Which countries are investing most aggressively?
- Which segments are being prioritized?
- Are policies creating real capacity or only political messaging?
- Which firms benefit most from public support?
- How do subsidies affect global competition?

---

## Strategic Relevance

Industrial policy is now one of the defining forces in the semiconductor sector.

It shapes:

- where chips are produced
- which firms expand
- how supply chains evolve
- how governments compete for strategic technologies
