# Semiconductor Supply Chain Overview

The semiconductor supply chain is a highly complex, globalized system involving multiple stages, specialized actors, and critical dependencies.

Understanding this structure is essential for analyzing technological competition, economic security, and geopolitical risk.

---

## Supply Chain Stages

### 1. Design (Fabless Companies)

Companies design semiconductor architectures and chips without manufacturing them.

**Key actors:**
- NVIDIA
- AMD
- Qualcomm
- Broadcom
- MediaTek

**Key dependencies:**
- advanced design tools (EDA software)
- access to leading-edge foundries

---

### 2. Manufacturing (Foundries)

Foundries manufacture chips designed by fabless firms.

**Key actors:**
- TSMC
- Samsung Electronics
- Intel
- SMIC
- GlobalFoundries

**Key dependencies:**
- advanced equipment
- high-purity materials
- industrial gases

---

### 3. Equipment

Specialized machinery used to manufacture chips.

**Key actors:**
- ASML (lithography)
- Applied Materials
- Lam Research
- KLA Corporation
- Tokyo Electron

**Key dependencies:**
- precision engineering
- supply chains of components and optics

---

### 4. Materials

Critical inputs required for semiconductor production.

**Includes:**
- silicon and wafers
- gallium and germanium
- rare earth elements
- industrial gases (neon, helium, argon)
- specialty chemicals

**Key risks:**
- geographic concentration
- export restrictions
- supply disruptions

---

### 5. Packaging and Testing

Final stage where chips are assembled and tested before distribution.

**Key regions:**
- Southeast Asia
- China
- Taiwan

**Key dependencies:**
- labor
- logistics
- advanced packaging technologies

---

## Key Chokepoints

Several bottlenecks define the global semiconductor system:

- advanced lithography (EUV)
- leading-edge foundry capacity
- critical materials (gallium, neon)
- semiconductor equipment
- advanced packaging capabilities

Control over these chokepoints provides strategic leverage.

---

## Global Structure

The semiconductor supply chain is geographically distributed:

- United States → design, software, equipment
- Taiwan → advanced manufacturing
- South Korea → memory and advanced chips
- Japan → materials and equipment
- China → manufacturing scale and demand
- Europe → equipment and specialized inputs

---

## OSINT Indicators to Monitor

Key signals across the supply chain:

- new fab construction and expansion
- export controls affecting equipment or materials
- supply disruptions (conflicts, natural disasters)
- shifts in trade flows
- corporate investments and partnerships
- industrial policy announcements

---

## Analytical Implications

The semiconductor supply chain is:

- highly interdependent
- difficult to replicate domestically
- vulnerable to geopolitical shocks

This creates:

- strategic competition between major powers
- incentives for reshoring and diversification
- increasing fragmentation of global supply chains

---

## Strategic Relevance

The semiconductor supply chain is a central element of:

- global technological competition
- economic security strategies
- military and dual-use capabilities
- digital infrastructure

Understanding its structure is essential for OSINT analysis.
